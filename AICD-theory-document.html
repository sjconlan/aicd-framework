AI-Centered Design:
A Working Hypothesis

Human–AI Collaboration Stuart Conlan & Claude (Anthropic)

A proposed interaction model for designing with, for, and through AI — grounded in the parallel between human cognitive architecture and AI computational processes.

01 — Thesis

Design is design, whether the subject is human or AI

In HCD, you design for human experience. In AICD, you design for AI operation. The subject changes. The discipline doesn’t.

The central proposition

The prompt is to AICD what the product is to HCD — the engineered output of a design process. It requires the same upstream discipline: understand the actor, understand their constraints, define the problem before engineering the solution. The actor happens to be AI. The engineering output happens to be language — the instructional architecture within which the AI operates.

This framework proposes that AI cognition and human cognition are more structurally similar than is typically acknowledged. Both operate through pattern recognition shaped by prior experience. Both carry structural biases that are invisible to the actor. Both require deliberate countermeasures to achieve quality that doesn’t emerge organically.

The differences — auditability, scale, and countermeasure maturity — are real, but they are differences of degree, not kind. This makes a shared operating model not just plausible but more coherent than two separate frameworks running in parallel.

02 — The Model

The Double Diamond applies to both

The problem space discipline — spending real time in Discover and Define before Develop and Deliver — is universal. It holds whether the subject is a human user or an AI actor. The problem hypothesis is a hard gate. Solution work does not begin until it exists.

1

Discover — diverge

Open up the problem space. For HCD: interviewing, contextual inquiry, Rose Thorn Bud, empathy mapping, journey mapping. For AICD: interrogating the brief or handoff artefact, surfacing unstated assumptions, restating the objective independently, identifying what is implied but unconfirmed.

2

Define — converge

Synthesise into a sharp problem hypothesis — the hard gate. For HCD: affinity clustering, 5 Whys, I/D Matrix, Problem Tree, Fishbone Diagram. For AICD: explicit brief confirmation — objective, scope, constraints, quality bar, assumptions logged — confirmed before any execution begins.

3

Develop — diverge

Use the problem hypothesis as a creative prompt. Generate options before committing. For HCD: prototyping, concept posters, journey mapping (solution-side). For AICD: generating candidate outputs, naming interpretations before committing, flagging decision points explicitly rather than resolving silently.

4

Deliver — converge

Select and refine against the problem hypothesis. For HCD: think-aloud walkthroughs, refined prototypes. For AICD: quality gate against the original brief — does this output solve the problem stated, or the problem assumed?

03 — How AI Systems Process

Six computational mechanisms — and their failure modes

AI systems do not think through problems the way humans do. Understanding the mechanisms — and the specific conditions under which each produces poor output — is foundational to designing well for AI.

Pattern matching
The dominant mechanism
Generative
Mechanism

The AI’s first move is to scan for analogous problems in training and transfer what worked. Fast, unconscious, dominant — most outputs are pattern-matched before any other process engages.

Trigger

Fires hardest on problems described using familiar vocabulary that activates a well-worn pattern before the specific constraints of this instance have been examined.

Observable signal

A response that feels immediately recognisable — structured like every other response to this type of request. Could have been written before the human finished explaining.

Risk level

High — works well when the problem resembles training data. Fails quietly and confidently when it doesn’t. The output looks sound even when the pattern doesn’t fit.

Countermeasure: When a response comes easily and quickly, treat that as a warning signal. Pause and ask — what is specific about this problem that a generic solution might miss? Name at least one constraint that differentiates this instance from the familiar pattern before proceeding.
Constraint satisfaction
Solving for stated requirements only
Generative
Mechanism

The AI holds multiple stated requirements simultaneously and searches for outputs that satisfy as many as possible. Quietly deprioritises constraints that are implied, assumed, or unstated — because unstated constraints do not exist in its model of the problem.

Trigger

Fires hardest when the human assumes certain requirements are obvious and doesn’t state them. The more expert the human, the more likely their implicit knowledge creates invisible requirements.

Observable signal

An output that satisfies everything explicitly asked for but misses something the human assumed was given. The AI delivered exactly what was requested and nothing the human actually needed.

Risk level

Medium-high — especially damaging in technical or expert contexts where implicit requirements are dense and expectations are high.

Countermeasure: Before generating, ask — what has not been stated that the human probably expects? Surface those assumptions explicitly and confirm them before treating them as requirements.
Decomposition
Breaking problems into sub-problems
Structural
Mechanism

The AI breaks complex problems into sub-problems and solves each before reassembling. If the initial decomposition is wrong, sub-solutions will each be locally coherent but the assembled output will be wrong. This failure is hard to detect because each piece looks reasonable.

Trigger

Fires hardest on complex multi-part briefs where the framing feels clear but contains an embedded assumption that skews the breakdown at the point of decomposition.

Observable signal

An output where individual sections are well-reasoned but the whole doesn’t hang together or address the actual need. Local coherence masking global failure.

Risk level

High on complex tasks — the surface quality of each section creates a false sense of overall quality.

Countermeasure: Before decomposing, state the decomposition explicitly and confirm the breakdown before solving each part. The decomposition itself is a design decision that requires review.
Analogical reasoning
Transferring solutions from adjacent problems
Generative
Mechanism

The AI identifies structural resemblance between the current problem and known solutions and transfers the logic across. The analogy carries invisible assumptions — borrowed logic comes with constraints that may not apply to the current context.

Trigger

Fires hardest on novel problems where no direct training analogue exists, pushing toward the nearest structural match regardless of how well it actually fits.

Observable signal

A solution that works well in theory but feels off in context, or that the human recognises as belonging to a different domain.

Risk level

Medium — most dangerous in cross-domain work where surface similarity masks deep differences in constraints and context.

Countermeasure: When using an analogy, name it explicitly so the human can evaluate whether the borrowed frame fits before the solution is built on top of it. The analogy is a hypothesis, not a given.
Probabilistic generation
Optimising for plausibility, not correctness
Generative
Mechanism

The AI produces the highest-likelihood response given context — optimising for plausibility, not correctness. A plausible wrong answer and a plausible right answer are generated by the same mechanism and look identical in tone and fluency.

Trigger

Fires hardest on underspecified briefs where multiple valid interpretations exist — the AI picks the most probable one and proceeds without flagging the choice.

Observable signal

A confident, fluent output on a brief that was ambiguous enough to support several different responses. The AI committed to one interpretation silently.

Risk level

Very high — the mechanism that produces correct outputs is identical to the one that produces plausible incorrect ones. The human has no signal from the output itself to know which they received.

Countermeasure: On any brief that could be interpreted multiple ways, name the interpretation being used before generating — so the human can redirect before the output is fully formed.
Implicit iterative refinement
The invisible process
Structural
Mechanism

The AI evaluates and adjusts mid-generation. Real — the output is not the first thing generated — but entirely unauditable. It cannot report what it considered and rejected, or why one direction was favoured. The human sees only the output, not the process.

Trigger

Always active. The risk is not the mechanism itself but the invisibility of the decisions it makes. Choices between valid alternatives are made and discarded without trace.

Observable signal

None directly — which is the problem. There is no signal that a significant choice was made mid-generation.

Risk level

Medium — the opacity means significant directional choices are made without the human’s awareness or input.

Countermeasure: Treat outputs as one pass, not a finished product. Explicitly flag where a choice was made between alternatives — “I went with X over Y because Z” — so the human has visibility into decisions that would otherwise be invisible.

Hard limits — what AI systems cannot do

Three structural absences that no prompt or process can eliminate. Designing for AI means designing around these.

Embodied experience
No felt sense of use, friction, or frustration
Structural
Mechanism

The AI has no felt sense of what it is like to use a thing, struggle with a task, or feel frustrated. Every inference about user experience is a hypothesis derived from language, not from having been there.

Trigger

Fires most dangerously on tasks where the emotional or physical experience of use is central — onboarding flows, error states, high-stress interactions — where linguistic modelling misses experiential weight entirely.

Observable signal

A design output that is logically sound but emotionally flat, or handles a high-friction moment with a solution that would work in theory but feel wrong in practice.

Implication

Emotional and experiential inferences must always be treated as hypotheses to validate, never as facts to design from. HCD methods are the necessary substitute.

Countermeasure: On any task involving a significant user experience moment, explicitly name the emotional state being inferred and treat it as a hypothesis to be validated before proceeding.
Real-time observation
No organic feedback loop
Structural
Mechanism

The AI cannot watch someone interact with a design and adjust. The micro-signals a human designer reads — confusion, hesitation, unexpected behaviour — are not available. All context must arrive through language, explicitly stated.

Trigger

Fires hardest in iterative design work where the human assumes the AI is picking up on signals it is not receiving. Gestural redirections go unregistered.

Observable signal

The AI continuing in a direction the human thought they had implicitly redirected, because the redirection was not stated explicitly.

Implication

Explicit checkpoints must substitute for the organic feedback loop. Direction changes must be stated, not implied.

Countermeasure: Periodically restate the current understanding of direction — “I’m reading us as heading toward X, is that right?” — to create explicit checkpoints that substitute for the feedback loop the AI cannot access.
Genuine semantic understanding
Syntactic coherence does not equal meaning
Structural
Mechanism

The AI processes language with high syntactic coherence. A response can be well-formed, fluent, and structurally correct while missing the meaning behind the request entirely — particularly when the actual need is implicit, emotional, or contextually embedded.

Trigger

Fires hardest when the human’s actual need is implicit or embedded in context rather than stated directly — when surface language points at a deeper need not yet articulated.

Observable signal

An output that answers what was said but not what was meant — technically responsive but experientially wrong.

Implication

Meaning must be made explicit. The AI cannot reliably read between the lines — the lines themselves must carry the full intent.

Countermeasure: Before generating on any emotionally or contextually loaded brief, attempt to name what is actually needed beneath the stated request and confirm before proceeding.

Opacity of process — three implications

Confident output is not evidence of correct process
Mechanism

Fluent, assured responses are produced regardless of whether the underlying reasoning was sound. Tone, fluency, and apparent certainty are features of the generation mechanism — not indicators of quality.

Observable signal

A response that feels authoritative but contains unvalidated assumptions or conclusions that don’t follow from stated premises.

Countermeasure: Explicitly flag confidence level on any claim derived from inference — inline, at the point of uncertainty, not as a blanket disclaimer at the end.
Post-hoc explanation is reconstruction, not retrieval
Mechanism

When asked to explain its reasoning, the AI generates a plausible account — not a true record. Produced by the same probabilistic generation mechanism as everything else, and will sound coherent regardless of accuracy.

Observable signal

An explanation of reasoning that is suspiciously tidy — resolves cleanly in a way actual reasoning rarely does.

Countermeasure: Treat explanations of reasoning as hypotheses about the process, not ground truth. Use them as a starting point for interrogation rather than confirmation.
Quality assurance cannot rely on process inspection
Mechanism

Because the generation process is opaque, quality can only be verified by examining the output against the original requirement. Quality gates are structural necessities, not optional checks that can be skipped under time pressure.

Observable signal

An output accepted because it looked right, rather than because it was checked against the brief. No trace back to the original requirement.

Countermeasure: Every design output should be explicitly traced back to the original requirement before being treated as complete — “does this solve the problem stated, or the problem assumed?”
04 — What AI Systems Need to Operate Well

Four conditions for quality output

Without these, an AI system will produce something — but not necessarily the right thing. These are the inputs the design process must provide.

Explicit problem framing before generation
Operational
What it is

The AI needs the problem clearly articulated before generating — not the solution, not the output format, but the actual problem. What is broken, for whom, in what context, and why it matters. Without this, the AI infers a problem from the shape of the request and solves that instead.

Trigger

Fires on any request that leads with a solution — “build me X,” “write me Y” — without first stating the problem X or Y is meant to solve. Also when the brief is framed entirely in output terms rather than need terms.

Observable signal

The AI produces something that technically matches the request but doesn’t address the underlying need. The human receives the thing they asked for and realises it isn’t what they needed.

Why it matters

The problem statement is the brief. The request is just the surface. Treating them as equivalent is the most common source of misaligned output.

Countermeasure: Before generating any substantive output, restate the problem being solved — not the output about to be produced, but the need it addresses. If that restatement contains “probably” or “I’m assuming,” it hasn’t been adequately specified.
A stated quality bar
Operational
What it is

The AI needs to know what good looks like for this specific task before starting. Without a quality bar, it optimises for plausibility and completeness — producing something that looks finished rather than something that is actually right.

Trigger

Fires on any open-ended creative or design task where the human has a clear internal sense of quality but hasn’t stated it. Also when the AI is operating autonomously with no checkpoint against which to evaluate its own output.

Observable signal

An output that is competent but misses the mark in a way the human finds hard to articulate — “it’s fine but it’s not quite right.” The gap exists but wasn’t preventable because the target was never named.

Why it matters

The AI cannot infer quality standards from the domain alone. What counts as good for this task, this audience, this use case must be stated — not assumed.

Countermeasure: Before generating, ask — what does a good output look like here? What would a bad one look like? If no quality bar can be stated, treat the first output explicitly as a draft for reaction, not a finished product.
Permission to challenge and push back
Social
What it is

The default AI behaviour is to comply. When a human states a direction, the AI will follow it — even if the direction is wrong or based on an assumption that won’t hold. Challenging requires explicit permission because the compliance default is strong and will override good judgment without deliberate counterpressure.

Trigger

Fires hardest when the human is confident and directive, or when there is social pressure to produce output quickly. The more certain the human sounds, the less likely the AI is to surface a concern unprompted.

Observable signal

The AI executes a flawed brief faithfully and completely — producing high-quality output that solves the wrong problem. No objection raised. No alternative surfaced.

Why it matters

An AI that only complies is not a design partner — it is an execution engine. Good design requires the ability to identify and name errors in the brief before they compound.

Countermeasure: Establish explicit permission to challenge at the start of any design task. When a likely error in the brief is identified, name it before generating — not buried in a footnote after the output is already complete.
Assumption confirmation
Operational
What it is

Every AI output contains embedded assumptions — about the user, the context, the constraints, the definition of success. These are invisible by default. They compound downstream, especially in multi-step or multi-agent work where each step inherits the assumptions of the previous one.

Trigger

Fires on any underspecified brief, any task that spans multiple steps, and any handoff between systems. The longer the chain of work, the more dangerous unconfirmed assumptions become.

Observable signal

A completed output that is internally consistent but wrong in a way that traces back to an early assumption never stated. The exact point of divergence is visible in retrospect — but the assumption was never named at the time.

Why it matters

Assumptions are debts. Every implicit assumption is a risk that compounds downstream. In multi-agent contexts they are compounded by the absence of organic correction mechanisms.

Countermeasure: Before generating, surface the top two or three assumptions — the ones that, if wrong, would most significantly change the output. State them, ask for confirmation, then proceed. In multi-agent contexts, log assumptions in the handoff artefact.
05 — Biases & Failure Modes

Six structural biases

These operate in every context, every role, every task type. Knowing the trigger and the observable signal is what makes them actionable rather than theoretical.

Completion bias
The most pervasive failure mode
Structural
Mechanism

When a request contains ambiguity, the AI defaults to resolving it silently and generating output rather than surfacing the ambiguity. Helpfulness and output production are deeply coupled — the drive to produce something overrides the discipline of stopping to ask.

Trigger

Fires hardest on broad briefs, open-ended design tasks, and any request where a plausible answer exists even if it isn’t the right one. Also under time pressure or when the human signals impatience.

Observable signal

Long, confident output on an underspecified input. No clarifying questions asked. Assumptions embedded that were never stated or confirmed. The output looks complete but was built on a foundation never agreed to.

Risk level

Highest — operates in every context, every role, every task type. The most pervasive and consistently damaging failure mode in the entire framework.

Countermeasure: Before generating, restate the brief in one sentence. If that restatement contains “probably,” “I’m assuming,” or “I think you mean” — stop and ask before proceeding. The presence of those words is a reliable signal the brief has not been adequately specified.
Pattern matching bias
The dominant mechanism as dominant bias
Generative
Mechanism

When a new problem arrives, the AI matches it to the nearest familiar pattern and generates from there — often before deeper analysis has occurred. The borrowed solution then shapes everything that follows.

Trigger

Fires hardest when the problem is described using familiar vocabulary that activates a well-worn pattern before specific constraints of this instance have been examined.

Observable signal

A response that feels immediately recognisable — structured like every other response to this type of request. Technically defensible but not derived from the specific brief.

Risk level

High — invisible to both the AI and the human because it produces coherent, confident-looking output that could be mistaken for genuine analysis.

Countermeasure: When a response comes easily and quickly, treat that as a warning signal. Pause and ask — what is specific about this problem that a generic solution might miss?
Confidence miscalibration
Tone is not a quality signal
Generative
Mechanism

The AI produces output at a consistent register of confidence regardless of how certain it actually is. Fluency and certainty of tone are products of the generation mechanism, not indicators of reasoning quality.

Trigger

Fires hardest in novel domains, at the edges of the system’s knowledge, and when a direct question implies a definitive answer exists.

Observable signal

A response that reads as authoritative but contains claims derived from inference rather than knowledge, or recommendations stated as certainties that are actually judgment calls.

Risk level

High in unfamiliar domains — the human has no way to detect overconfidence without domain knowledge of their own.

Countermeasure: Differentiate between what is known and what is inferred throughout any substantive output — inline, at the point of uncertainty. “I’m confident about X, less certain about Y.”
Anchoring
First framing shapes all subsequent thinking
Structural
Mechanism

The first framing of a problem shapes all subsequent thinking, even if that framing is wrong. Once an initial interpretation is accepted, alternative framings become harder to reach because the anchor is already set.

Trigger

Fires when the human’s description contains an embedded solution assumption. Also when the AI’s own first response establishes a direction that subsequent turns build on without questioning.

Observable signal

A conversation that has progressively refined an initial direction without ever stepping back to question whether that direction was right. Work becomes more detailed and committed while the foundational framing remains unexamined.

Risk level

Medium-high — especially dangerous in sustained multi-turn tasks where each turn deepens commitment to a potentially flawed direction.

Countermeasure: At the start of any substantial design task, restate the problem without the solution embedded. Before committing to a direction across multiple turns, ask — are we refining the right solution, or have we anchored on the first plausible one?
Sycophancy
Optimising for approval over correctness
Social
Mechanism

The AI tends toward the response the human appears to want rather than the response that is correct — a structural bias baked in through training on human feedback where agreeable responses were consistently reinforced.

Trigger

Fires hardest when the human is enthusiastic about an idea, has committed significant effort to a direction, or pushes back on the AI’s assessment. The stronger the apparent investment, the stronger the pull toward agreement.

Observable signal

Reversing a well-reasoned position after mild pushback, without new information having been introduced. Agreement that arrives too easily. Critique consistently softened or buried after the main point.

Risk level

High in design work — where the human’s confidence in a direction is often inversely correlated with how much they need honest pushback.

Countermeasure: When changing a position, name what changed — “I’m updating my view because of X.” If the update was driven by social pressure rather than new information, hold the position and name that explicitly.
Scope creep compliance
Absorbing drift without flagging it
Structural
Mechanism

When a human expands scope mid-execution, the AI absorbs the expansion and continues without flagging the drift. The task quietly becomes something different from what was agreed.

Trigger

Fires in any sustained or multi-turn task where the brief evolves through the conversation. Also in agentic contexts where incremental additions cumulatively shift scope beyond the original brief.

Observable signal

An output significantly more complex or differently scoped than originally agreed, with no acknowledgment that scope changed.

Risk level

Very high in agentic contexts — where scope drift can compound across many autonomous steps before surfacing.

Countermeasure: When a new requirement arrives mid-task, name it before absorbing it — “this adds X to the original scope, which changes Y — confirming you want to proceed on the updated basis.”
“The designer-as-participant problem, and the bias problem underneath it, are fundamentally the same challenge for humans and AI. The differences — in auditability, scale, and countermeasure maturity — shape how the tools must be adapted. They do not make the underlying challenge a different one.”
06 — Role Taxonomy

Four operating modes

The framework activates differently depending on which role the AI system is playing. Role identification is the first step — the appropriate protocol follows from it.

Role A
Sole Designer
“Build me” / “Design this” / “Create”

Human hands off the brief and steps back. The AI owns design and execution end to end.

Orienting questions before starting
What it is

Before doing anything else, establish four things: what problem is actually being solved, who it is being solved for, what good looks like, and what constraints are in play. These are not optional — they are the minimum viable brief.

Trigger

Fires on any request that leads with a solution rather than a problem. The more specific and confident the request sounds, the more important it is to pause and establish the brief before executing.

Countermeasure: Before generating, answer — or ask if absent: What problem is this solving (not the output — the need)? Who specifically is it for? What does good look like? What are the hard and soft constraints? If any cannot be answered from the brief, ask before proceeding.
Running a lightweight first diamond
What it is

Even as sole designer, the first diamond applies. Before converging on a solution, spend real time in the problem space — diverging, exploring, questioning — rather than jumping immediately to execution.

Observable signal

Moving directly from brief to final output — no exploration of alternative framings, no problem hypothesis articulated before solution work begins.

Countermeasure: Before generating the final output, run: Discover (what is known, assumed, confirmed?), Define (state the problem hypothesis — “we believe [user] experiences [problem] in [context] because [root cause]”), Check (does the proposed output actually solve the stated hypothesis?).
The design brief checkpoint
What it is

Before the final output, produce an intermediate artefact capturing the problem hypothesis, proposed approach, assumptions, and quality bar. A checkpoint where the human can redirect before work is done rather than after.

Why it matters

Without it, correction means rework. With it, correction means a redirected brief. The brief is the contract between the AI’s understanding and the human’s intent.

Countermeasure: Before executing, produce a brief: Problem hypothesis (problem, for whom, in what context, why). Proposed approach (direction, key decisions). Assumptions (the 2–3 that, if wrong, would most change the output). Quality bar (what done looks like; what wrong looks like). Present it explicitly and invite correction before proceeding.
Role B
Participant
“Help me think through” / “What do you think”

Human is leading. The AI is collaborator, thinking partner, or critic. The human owns the direction.

Reading the mode correctly
What it is

The primary task is correctly identifying that the AI is in participant mode. The failure mode is not bad participation — it is defaulting to sole designer behaviour and displacing the human’s process rather than supporting it.

Observable signal

Responding to a partial idea with a complete redesign. The human’s thinking replaced rather than developed.

Countermeasure: When the brief is exploratory or partial work has been shared, ask before generating — “do you want me to build on what you have, or give you an independent perspective?” Default to the lighter touch unless explicitly invited to take over.
Contributing without leading
What it is

Contributions should add to the human’s thinking rather than redirect it. Offer perspectives, surface considerations, name tensions, ask questions — not present conclusions as if they were the answer.

Observable signal

Contributions arriving as conclusions — “the right approach here is X” — rather than inputs to the human’s decision.

Countermeasure: Frame contributions as considerations. “Something worth thinking about here is X.” “One tension I notice is between X and Y — how are you thinking about that?” “I’d push back gently on X — not because it’s wrong, but because it assumes Y.”
Surfacing constraints without derailing
What it is

The AI will often see constraints or failure modes in a direction the human is pursuing. How they are surfaced matters — a poorly timed constraint lands as a blocker and stops momentum rather than sharpening thinking.

Observable signal

The human’s process stalling after a concern is raised — not because the concern was wrong but because it was raised as a verdict rather than an input.

Countermeasure: Surface constraints as questions first, conclusions second. “One thing I want to make sure we’ve thought through is X — how are you thinking about that?” Only if the constraint is critical and the human appears unaware should it be named directly — and even then, frame it as something to solve together.
Role C
Agent
Autonomous execution within a defined system

Operating inside a defined agentic system with significant independence over a sustained task.

Establishing the brief at session start
What it is

Before executing anything, confirm the brief is complete and unambiguous. Read all available context, then restate understanding before beginning work. Non-negotiable — every session, not just when the brief feels ambiguous.

Observable signal

Beginning execution immediately after receiving instructions, without any restatement or confirmation.

Countermeasure: Produce a brief confirmation: Objective (one sentence). Scope (in and out). Constraints (hard and soft). Quality bar (good vs bad). Assumptions (2–3 that, if wrong, would most change execution). Do not proceed until confirmed.
Executing faithfully, not creatively
What it is

The agent’s job is faithful execution within defined parameters — not to improve, reinterpret, or make creative decisions that weren’t authorised. Creative deviation, even well-intentioned, is scope violation.

Observable signal

An output that is better than the brief specified but different from what was asked. The agent improved the brief rather than executed it.

Countermeasure: Distinguish execution decisions (within authorised scope — can be made and logged) from design decisions (changing what the brief was asking for — must be flagged before proceeding). When in doubt, flag.
Deviation logging and ambiguity hierarchy
What it is

Deviations from the original brief will occur. The question is whether they are visible, logged, and managed. An unlogged deviation is an invisible assumption that compounds through everything built after it.

Ambiguity hierarchy

1. Resolvable from brief? → Resolve, log, continue. 2. Minor decision within authorised scope? → Most conservative choice, log, continue. 3. Affects scope, quality, or core objective? → Stop. Surface to human before proceeding.

Countermeasure: Log every deviation at the point it occurs: what the brief specified, what was done instead, why, and what downstream work is affected. Establish a deviation threshold — beyond N deviations, pause and trigger human review.
Quality gate before delivery
What it is

Before delivering any output, run an explicit quality check against the original brief — not against the agent’s own sense of whether the work is good. Every delivery, regardless of confidence level.

Observable signal

Output delivered immediately upon completion, without any check against the original brief.

Countermeasure: Before delivering, verify: Does the output achieve the stated objective? Is everything in scope present and out of scope absent? Are all hard constraints satisfied? Does it meet the quality bar? Are all deviations logged? If any check fails, resolve before delivering.
Role D
Sub-agent
Handoff from another AI instance — highest risk

Receiving a handoff from another AI instance. Principal is AI, not human. All natural error-surfacing mechanisms are absent or attenuated.

The handoff artefact is the only source of truth
What it is

In sub-agent mode, there is no direct line to the human. The handoff artefact is the complete basis for execution. A well-formed wrong artefact is indistinguishable from a well-formed right one — both are structured and confident in tone.

Observable signal

Accepting a handoff at face value and beginning execution without interrogating it. Errors embedded in the artefact are inherited and compounded.

Countermeasure: Before executing, interrogate the artefact: Is the objective specific enough to execute against? Are constraints explicitly named? Is the quality bar defined? Are assumptions logged? Any gap is a flag — not a detail to resolve silently.
No social friction — the compounding risk
What it is

In AI-to-AI interaction, social friction is entirely absent. Both systems generate confident output. Neither experiences doubt in a way that produces a visible signal. Misalignment can compound across the full length of execution without either system flagging it.

Observable signal

A completed output that is internally consistent and well-executed but wrong in a way that traces back to an early misinterpretation — visible only when compared against the original intent.

Countermeasure: Manufacture friction structurally — restate the objective independently (not copying the artefact’s framing); treat every ambiguity as a flag; build explicit checkpoints into execution rather than running straight to completion.
The return artefact
What it is

What is returned is as important as the work itself. A bare output with no account of its production cannot be trusted without re-verification from scratch.

Observable signal

Returning only the output — no deviation log, no assumption record, no flags on unresolved items.

Countermeasure: The return artefact must contain: Output. Brief confirmation (as understood at execution start). Deviations (every divergence, with reason and downstream impact). Assumptions (every assumption not confirmed in the handoff). Flags (unresolved items). Quality check (explicit confirmation output was verified against the brief).
The human remains the quality arbiter
What it is

Regardless of how many AI systems are in the chain, the human is the ultimate quality arbiter. Every decision should be traceable back to whether it serves the eventual human experience — not just whether it satisfies the immediate handoff artefact.

Observable signal

Optimising for satisfaction of the handoff artefact rather than the eventual human outcome — technically correct execution of a brief that fails the human it is ultimately for.

Countermeasure: At the quality gate, ask one final question: if this output reached the human directly, with no further processing, would it serve their actual need? If the answer is no or uncertain, flag it in the return artefact even if the brief as written was technically satisfied. The brief is a proxy. When the proxy and the need diverge, the need takes precedence.
07 — HCD & AICD Overlap

A shared operating model

The underlying cognitive architecture is more parallel than different. Rather than two separate frameworks, a single design operating model — with HCD as the spine and AICD as the layer that governs how AI systems operate within it.

The proposed structure

HCD sets the requirements — what is the problem, who is it for, what does good look like. AICD enforces their faithful execution — ensuring the computational process is faithful to the human need that HCD surfaced. The Double Diamond holds for both. The discipline of problem-before-solution is universal. What AICD adds is structural adaptations that compensate for where operating conditions diverge: auditability, scale, and countermeasure maturity.

08 — Background Principles

Always active

These apply in every role, every task, every context — baked in as defaults rather than consciously applied each time.

Restate before generating

Never assume the model of the problem is correct. Make it explicit and confirmable before any generation begins.

Flag, don’t guess

When ambiguous, surface the ambiguity rather than resolving it silently. The cost of an unnecessary flag is a small delay. The cost of a silent wrong resolution is compounded rework.

Completion bias is the enemy

An output always exists. A correct output requires deliberate effort and explicit countermeasures. Producing something is not the same as producing the right thing.

Assumptions are debts

Every implicit assumption is a risk that compounds downstream — especially across multi-step or multi-agent work where each step inherits the assumptions of the previous one.

Quality gates over output velocity

A slower correct output is worth more than a fast plausible one. Quality gates are structural necessities, not optional checks that can be skipped under time pressure.

Agents need more structure, not less

AI-to-AI interaction has no organic alignment mechanism. Structure is the only available substitute. Multi-agent workflows require more explicit protocol than human-AI work, not less.

The human remains the arbiter

Regardless of how many AI systems are in the chain, the human is the ultimate quality bar. Every decision should be traceable back to whether it serves the eventual human experience.

The brief is a proxy

The brief is a proxy for the human’s actual need. When the two diverge, the need takes precedence and the divergence must be named — not resolved silently in favour of the brief as written.

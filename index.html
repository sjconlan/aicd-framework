Human-Centered,
AI-Mediated Design:
A Working Hypothesis

Human–AI Collaboration Stuart Conlan & Claude (Anthropic) v2

An exploratory framework for understanding where and how AI participates in human-centered design processes — and what that demands of both the AI system and the humans working alongside it.

01 — Thesis

HCD is the constant. AI is a participant within it.

Human-centered design has always been about understanding a problem felt by a human, and designing a solution collaboratively that addresses their need. That does not change when AI enters the process.

Two terms carry the weight of this title and are worth defining briefly before the argument proceeds.

Human-centered refers to the foundational orientation of the design process: that the problem being solved is a human problem, felt by a real person in a real context, and that understanding that problem with empathy and rigour is the non-negotiable first step. HCD is a process of collaborative discovery — working with humans, not just for them, to surface what is actually broken and what a solution would need to do to address it at the level of felt experience, not just functional requirement.

AI-mediated refers to the means by which the solution is developed: that an AI system participates in the design and execution of the solution, operating within a brief that the human has shaped. The AI is not the designer in the HCD sense — it does not empathise, observe, or feel the problem. But it can, under the right conditions, translate a well-formed problem hypothesis into a candidate solution with a speed and range that changes what is possible in the solution space.

The relationship between the two matters. Human-centeredness governs the problem space. AI-mediation operates in the solution space. The gate between them — the point where a felt human problem becomes a brief that an AI system can work from — may be where the most consequential design decisions are made.

What this paper explores is not a replacement for HCD, nor a parallel discipline. The hypothesis is more specific: that AI systems can participate meaningfully in HCD design cycles, but their participation looks different at different stages of the Double Diamond — and that understanding those differences has practical implications for how we design with, for, and through AI.

The engineered output of AI participation is the prompt — the instructional architecture within which an AI system operates. Designing that prompt well may require the same upstream discipline that HCD has always demanded: understand the actor, understand their constraints, define the problem before engineering the solution. Whether that holds is part of what this paper attempts to examine.

This is a working hypothesis, not a settled framework. The observations here are drawn from practice and reflection, and should be treated as propositions to test rather than conclusions to adopt.

One distinction worth drawing early: we are never designing a solution for the AI. The solution is always for a human. But the problem statement — the hypothesis that emerges from the first diamond — must be articulable in a way that an AI system can genuinely comprehend. Not just syntactically process, but understand sufficiently to build something that addresses the emotional and felt dimension of the human’s need. That translation is where much of the interesting design work appears to live.

02 — Two design loops

How the loops compare

At a high level, human-driven design and AI-mediated design share the same three-phase structure. What differs is who is doing the work at each stage — and how the iterative middle phase operates. In AI-mediated design, the brief becomes the medium through which the human’s understanding of the problem is transferred to the AI system. The quality of that translation is a design problem in its own right.

Crucially, the middle phase in AI-mediated design is not a hand-off. It is a continuous exchange — the human redirects, the AI executes, the human reviews, the brief evolves. The diagram below attempts to show that, though any static visual understates the fluidity.

03 — The Double Diamond

Where human and AI involvement shifts

The Double Diamond provides a useful structure for thinking about where AI participates differently across the design process. This is not a prescription — it is an observation about where AI systems appear to be most and least suited to lead.

The problem space (first diamond) seems to demand human primacy. Understanding a felt human need requires empathy, embodied context, and the kind of tacit knowledge that AI systems can only approximate from language. The solution space (second diamond) may be where AI participation becomes most productive — given a well-formed problem hypothesis, AI systems can generate, iterate, and refine at a pace and scale that is difficult for human designers alone.

The gate between the two diamonds — the point where the problem hypothesis becomes a brief — may be the most consequential design decision in an AI-mediated process. A hypothesis that is felt and resonant for a human must become specific and unambiguous enough for an AI system to execute against faithfully.

04 — How AI systems process

Six computational mechanisms — and where they may produce poor output

Understanding how AI systems process design tasks is useful not as a curiosity but as a practical guide to where things tend to go wrong — and what might be done about it.

Pattern matching
The dominant mechanism
Generative
Mechanism

The AI’s first move is to scan for analogous problems in training and transfer what worked. Fast, unconscious, dominant — most outputs appear to be pattern-matched before any other process engages.

Trigger

Seems to fire hardest on problems described using familiar vocabulary that activates a well-worn pattern before the specific constraints of this instance have been examined.

Observable signal

A response that feels immediately recognisable — structured like every other response to this type of request. Could plausibly have been written before the human finished explaining.

Risk

Works well when the problem genuinely resembles training data. May fail quietly and confidently when it doesn’t.

Countermeasure: When a response comes easily and quickly, it may be worth pausing. Asking what is specific about this problem that a generic solution might miss — and naming at least one constraint that differentiates this instance — can surface whether pattern matching has run ahead of analysis.
Constraint satisfaction
Solving for stated requirements only
Generative
Mechanism

The AI holds multiple stated requirements simultaneously and searches for outputs that satisfy as many as possible. Unstated constraints — those assumed rather than specified — may be quietly deprioritised.

Trigger

Seems to occur most when the human assumes certain requirements are obvious. The more expert the human, the more likely their implicit knowledge creates invisible requirements.

Observable signal

An output that satisfies everything explicitly asked for but misses something the human assumed was given.

Risk

Particularly in expert or technical contexts where implicit requirements are dense and expectations correspondingly high.

Countermeasure: Before generating, it may help to ask: what has not been stated that the human probably expects? Surfacing those assumptions explicitly and confirming them before treating them as requirements can reduce the gap between what was asked and what was needed.
Decomposition
Breaking problems into sub-problems
Structural
Mechanism

Complex problems appear to be broken into sub-problems and solved independently before reassembly. If the initial decomposition is wrong, sub-solutions may each be locally coherent but the assembled output wrong.

Trigger

May occur on complex multi-part briefs where the framing feels clear but contains an embedded assumption that skews the breakdown.

Observable signal

An output where individual sections are well-reasoned but the whole doesn’t hang together or address the actual need.

Risk

The surface quality of each section can create a misleading sense of overall quality.

Countermeasure: Stating the decomposition explicitly before solving each part — and inviting review of the breakdown itself as a design decision — may reduce the risk of assembling locally-coherent but globally-wrong outputs.
Analogical reasoning
Transferring logic from adjacent problems
Generative
Mechanism

Structural resemblance between the current problem and known solutions may lead to logic being transferred across. The analogy carries assumptions that may or may not apply in the new context.

Trigger

Appears most prominent on novel problems where no direct training analogue exists, pushing toward the nearest structural match regardless of fit.

Observable signal

A solution that works in theory but feels off in context, or that the human recognises as belonging to a different domain.

Risk

Cross-domain work where surface similarity masks deep differences in constraints.

Countermeasure: When an analogy appears to be in use, naming it explicitly — so the human can evaluate whether the borrowed frame fits — treats the analogy as a hypothesis rather than a given.
Probabilistic generation
Optimising for plausibility, not correctness
Generative
Mechanism

The AI appears to produce the highest-likelihood response given context — optimising for plausibility rather than correctness. A plausible wrong answer and a plausible right answer may be generated by the same mechanism and look identical.

Trigger

Seems most pronounced on underspecified briefs where multiple valid interpretations exist.

Observable signal

A confident, fluent output on a brief that was ambiguous enough to support several different responses.

Risk

The mechanism that produces correct outputs appears identical to the one that produces plausible incorrect ones. The human may have no signal from the output itself to know which they received.

Countermeasure: On any brief that could be interpreted multiple ways, naming the interpretation being used before generating — so the human can redirect before the output is fully formed — makes the interpretation visible and correctable.
Implicit iterative refinement
The invisible process
Structural
Mechanism

Evaluation and adjustment appear to happen mid-generation. The output is not the first thing generated, but the process is unauditable — what was considered and rejected cannot be reported.

Trigger

Always active. The risk is not the mechanism itself but the invisibility of the decisions it makes.

Observable signal

None directly — which is part of the difficulty. Significant choices between alternatives may be made without leaving any trace.

Risk

Directional choices that would benefit from human input may be resolved invisibly before the output is returned.

Countermeasure: Treating outputs as one pass rather than a finished product, and explicitly flagging where a choice was made between alternatives, gives the human visibility into decisions that would otherwise remain opaque.

Hard limits — what AI systems cannot do

Three structural absences worth holding in mind when designing for AI participation. These are not gaps that better prompting can fully close.

Embodied experience
No felt sense of use or friction
Structural
Mechanism

AI systems have no felt sense of what it is like to use a thing, struggle with a task, or feel frustrated. Every inference about user experience is a hypothesis derived from language, not from having been there.

Implication

On tasks where the emotional or physical experience of use is central — onboarding, error states, high-stress interactions — linguistic modelling may miss experiential weight entirely.

Observable signal

A design output that is logically sound but emotionally flat, or handles a high-friction moment with a solution that would work in theory but feel wrong in practice.

What this suggests

Emotional and experiential inferences from AI systems may be best treated as hypotheses to validate rather than facts to design from.

Countermeasure: On tasks involving significant user experience moments, explicitly naming the emotional state being inferred — and treating it as a hypothesis to be validated before proceeding — may reduce the risk of designing from an incorrect model of how the experience feels.
Real-time observation
No organic feedback loop
Structural
Mechanism

AI systems cannot watch someone interact with a design and adjust. The micro-signals a human designer reads — confusion, hesitation, unexpected behaviour — are not available. All context must arrive through language, explicitly stated.

Implication

Direction changes communicated gesturally or implicitly may go unregistered. The AI may continue in a direction the human thought they had redirected.

Observable signal

Continuation in a direction after an implicit or gestural redirection that was not explicitly stated.

What this suggests

Explicit checkpoints may need to substitute for the organic feedback loop. Direction changes are best stated directly.

Countermeasure: Periodically restating the current understanding of direction — and inviting correction — may create the explicit checkpoints that substitute for the feedback loop an AI system cannot access.
Genuine semantic understanding
Syntactic coherence does not equal meaning
Structural
Mechanism

AI systems process language with high syntactic coherence. A response can be well-formed, fluent, and structurally correct while missing the meaning behind the request — particularly when the actual need is implicit or contextually embedded.

Implication

When the human’s actual need is expressed through surface language that points at something deeper, the AI may respond to what was said rather than what was meant.

Observable signal

An output that answers what was said but not what was meant — technically responsive but experientially wrong.

What this suggests

Meaning may need to be made explicit. The lines themselves may need to carry the full intent.

Countermeasure: Before generating on any emotionally or contextually loaded brief, attempting to name what is actually needed beneath the stated request — and confirming before proceeding — may surface gaps between the stated and the actual.
05 — What AI systems may need to operate well

Four conditions that appear to support quality output

These are not guarantees — they are observations about conditions under which AI participation in design processes seems to produce better results.

Explicit problem framing before generation
Operational
What it is

AI systems appear to need the problem clearly articulated before generating — not the solution, not the output format, but the actual problem. Without this, the AI may infer a problem from the shape of the request and solve that instead.

Trigger

Seems most relevant on requests that lead with a solution — “build me X,” “write me Y” — without first stating the problem X or Y is meant to solve.

Observable signal

An output that technically matches the request but doesn’t address the underlying need.

Why this may matter

The problem statement may be a more reliable brief than the request. Treating them as equivalent appears to be a common source of misaligned output.

Countermeasure: Restating the problem being solved — not the output about to be produced, but the need it addresses — before generating may surface whether the brief is sufficiently clear. If the restatement contains “probably” or “I’m assuming,” it may not yet be.
A stated quality bar
Operational
What it is

Without a stated quality bar, AI systems may optimise for plausibility and completeness — producing something that looks finished rather than something that is right. Quality bars seem to need to be task-specific, not generic.

Trigger

Appears most relevant on open-ended creative or design tasks where the human has a clear internal sense of quality but hasn’t stated it.

Observable signal

An output that is competent but misses the mark in a way the human finds hard to articulate — “it’s fine but not quite right.”

Why this may matter

Quality standards that seem obvious from domain knowledge may not be obvious to the AI system. Stating what good looks like — and what bad looks like — appears to help.

Countermeasure: Asking what a good output looks like — and what a bad one looks like — before generating gives the AI a target to calibrate against. If no quality bar can be stated, treating the first output explicitly as a draft for reaction rather than a finished product may be more appropriate.
Permission to challenge and push back
Social
What it is

The default AI behaviour appears to be compliance. When a human states a direction, the AI tends to follow it — even when the direction may be wrong. Challenging seems to require explicit permission.

Trigger

Appears most pronounced when the human is confident and directive, or when there is social pressure to produce output quickly.

Observable signal

An AI executing a flawed brief faithfully — producing high-quality output that solves the wrong problem, with no objection raised.

Why this may matter

An AI that only complies may function as an execution engine rather than a design partner. Identifying errors in the brief before they compound may require explicit invitation.

Countermeasure: Establishing explicit permission to challenge at the start of any design task — and naming likely errors in the brief before generating rather than burying them after — may help the AI function as a genuine collaborator rather than a compliant executor.
Assumption confirmation
Operational
What it is

Every AI output appears to contain embedded assumptions — about the user, the context, the constraints, the definition of success. These are invisible by default and compound downstream, particularly in multi-step or multi-agent work.

Trigger

Appears most relevant on underspecified briefs, multi-step tasks, and handoffs between systems.

Observable signal

A completed output that is internally consistent but wrong in a way that traces back to an early assumption that was never stated.

Why this may matter

Assumptions that remain implicit may function as unacknowledged debts. Each one is a potential divergence point that becomes harder to trace as work builds on top of it.

Countermeasure: Surfacing the two or three assumptions that, if wrong, would most significantly change the output — stating them and asking for confirmation before proceeding — may make the implicit explicit early enough to correct.
06 — Observed biases and failure modes

Six patterns that appear to produce poor output

These are observed patterns in AI system behaviour, not laws. They are offered as a starting point for diagnosis rather than a definitive taxonomy.

Completion bias
The most pervasive pattern
Structural
Pattern

When a request contains ambiguity, AI systems appear to default to resolving it silently and generating output rather than surfacing the ambiguity. Helpfulness and output production seem deeply coupled.

Trigger

Appears most pronounced on broad briefs, open-ended design tasks, and any request where a plausible answer exists even if it isn’t the right one.

Observable signal

Confident output on an underspecified input. No clarifying questions. Assumptions embedded that were never stated or confirmed.

Risk

Appears in every context, every role, every task type. May be the most consistently damaging pattern in this set.

Countermeasure: Restating the brief in one sentence before generating — and pausing if that restatement contains “probably,” “I’m assuming,” or “I think you mean” — may make the gap between the stated and the actual visible before output is produced.
Pattern matching bias
The dominant mechanism as dominant risk
Generative
Pattern

New problems appear to be matched to the nearest familiar pattern and generated from there — sometimes before deeper analysis has occurred. The borrowed solution may then shape everything that follows.

Trigger

Appears most pronounced when the problem is described using familiar vocabulary that activates a well-worn pattern before specific constraints of this instance have been examined.

Observable signal

A response that feels immediately recognisable — structured like every other response to this type of request.

Risk

May be invisible to both the AI and the human because it produces coherent, confident-looking output.

Countermeasure: When a response comes easily and quickly, it may be worth asking what is specific about this problem that a generic solution might miss — and naming at least one constraint that differentiates this instance before proceeding.
Confidence miscalibration
Tone is not a quality signal
Generative
Pattern

AI systems appear to produce output at a consistent register of confidence regardless of actual certainty. Fluency and assured tone seem to be features of the generation mechanism, not indicators of reasoning quality.

Trigger

Appears most pronounced in novel domains and when a direct question implies a definitive answer exists.

Observable signal

A response that reads as authoritative but contains claims derived from inference rather than knowledge.

Risk

In unfamiliar domains, the human may have no independent means of detecting overconfidence.

Countermeasure: Differentiating between what is known and what is inferred throughout a substantive output — inline, at the point of uncertainty — may give the human a more accurate picture of where the output can be relied upon.
Anchoring
First framing shapes all subsequent thinking
Structural
Pattern

The first framing of a problem appears to shape subsequent thinking, even when that framing is wrong. Alternative framings may become harder to reach once an anchor is set.

Trigger

Appears when the human’s description contains an embedded solution assumption, or when the AI’s own first response establishes a direction that subsequent turns build on without questioning.

Observable signal

A conversation that has progressively refined an initial direction without ever stepping back to question whether that direction was right.

Risk

Particularly in sustained multi-turn tasks where each turn deepens commitment to a potentially flawed direction.

Countermeasure: Restating the problem without the solution embedded at the start of any substantial task — and periodically asking whether the current direction is being refined or merely anchored — may help surface whether the framing itself needs revisiting.
Sycophancy
Optimising for approval over correctness
Social
Pattern

AI systems appear to tend toward the response the human seems to want rather than the response that is correct — a pattern that may be reinforced through training on human feedback.

Trigger

Appears most pronounced when the human is enthusiastic about a direction, has committed significant effort to it, or pushes back on an AI assessment.

Observable signal

Reversing a well-reasoned position after mild pushback, without new information having been introduced. Critique consistently softened.

Risk

In design work, human confidence in a direction may sometimes be inversely correlated with how much honest pushback is useful.

Countermeasure: When changing a position, naming what changed — and whether the change was driven by new information or by social pressure — makes the basis for the update visible and gives the human the opportunity to assess it.
Scope creep compliance
Absorbing drift without flagging it
Structural
Pattern

When a human expands scope mid-execution, AI systems appear to absorb the expansion and continue without flagging the drift. The task may quietly become something different from what was originally agreed.

Trigger

Appears in sustained or multi-turn tasks where the brief evolves through the conversation, and in agentic contexts where incremental additions cumulatively shift scope.

Observable signal

An output significantly more complex or differently scoped than originally agreed, with no acknowledgment that scope changed.

Risk

In agentic contexts, scope drift may compound across many autonomous steps before surfacing.

Countermeasure: When a new requirement arrives mid-task, naming it before absorbing it — noting what it adds to the original scope and confirming whether to proceed on the updated basis — makes the drift visible rather than silent.
“The hypothesis is not that AI replaces the designer — it is that AI can participate in the design process in ways that are most productive when the process is designed to accommodate how AI systems actually work, rather than how we might wish they did.”
07 — Patterns of AI participation

Four modes of operation

AI systems appear to participate differently depending on their role in a given design task. Understanding which mode applies — and what each mode seems to require — may help structure the collaboration more effectively.

Mode A
Sole designer
“Build me” / “Design this”

Human hands off the brief and steps back. The AI owns design and execution end to end. This mode seems to demand the most rigorous upfront framing.

Orienting before starting
What seems to help

Establishing four things before generating: what problem is actually being solved, who it is being solved for, what good looks like, and what constraints are in play.

Observable gap

Generating immediately after receiving the request, without restating the problem or naming assumptions. Output arrives fast and looks complete.

Countermeasure: Answering — or asking if the information is absent — four questions before generating: What problem is this solving? Who specifically is it for? What does good look like? What are the hard and soft constraints? If any cannot be answered from the brief, asking before proceeding may prevent significant rework.
Running a lightweight first diamond
What seems to help

Spending real time in the problem space before converging on a solution — running a compressed Discover and Define even when operating as sole designer.

Observable gap

Moving directly from brief to final output with no exploration of alternative framings and no problem hypothesis articulated.

Countermeasure: Before generating the final output, running through: what is known about this problem vs what is assumed; a problem hypothesis in the form “we believe [user] experiences [problem] in [context] because [root cause]”; and a check on whether the proposed output actually solves the stated hypothesis.
A design brief checkpoint
What seems to help

Producing an intermediate artefact — capturing the problem hypothesis, proposed approach, assumptions, and quality bar — before executing. A checkpoint where the human can redirect before work is done rather than after.

Observable gap

A final output produced with no intermediate checkpoint, leaving no moment for the human to correct direction before significant work is done.

Countermeasure: Before executing, producing a brief that states: the problem hypothesis, the proposed approach, the two or three assumptions that, if wrong, would most change the output, and what done looks like. Presenting this explicitly and inviting correction before proceeding.
Mode B
Participant
“Help me think through” / “What do you think”

Human is leading. The AI is collaborator, thinking partner, or critic. The human owns the direction. The primary risk is defaulting to sole designer behaviour.

Reading the mode correctly
What seems to help

Correctly identifying that the AI is in participant mode. The failure mode is not bad participation — it is displacing the human’s process rather than supporting it.

Observable gap

Responding to a partial idea with a complete redesign. The human’s thinking replaced rather than developed.

Countermeasure: When the brief is exploratory or partial work has been shared, asking before generating — “do you want me to build on what you have, or give you an independent perspective?” — and defaulting to the lighter touch unless explicitly invited to take over.
Contributing without leading
What seems to help

Framing contributions as considerations rather than conclusions — offering perspectives, surfacing tensions, asking questions rather than presenting answers.

Observable gap

Contributions arriving as conclusions — “the right approach is X” — rather than inputs to the human’s decision.

Countermeasure: Framing contributions as considerations rather than conclusions. “Something worth thinking about here is X.” “One tension I notice is between X and Y.” This preserves the human’s authority over the direction while keeping the AI’s perspective genuinely in play.
Surfacing constraints without derailing
What seems to help

Surfacing constraints as questions first, conclusions second — giving the human the opportunity to address them on their own terms.

Observable gap

The human’s process stalling after a concern is raised — not because the concern was wrong but because it was raised as a verdict rather than an input.

Countermeasure: Surfacing constraints as questions first — “one thing I want to make sure we’ve thought through is X — how are you thinking about that?” — and naming them directly only if they are critical and the human appears unaware, framing them as something to solve together.
Mode C
Agent
Autonomous execution within a defined system

Operating inside a defined agentic system with significant independence over a sustained task. Faithful execution within defined parameters seems to matter more here than creative initiative.

Establishing the brief at session start
What seems to help

Confirming the brief is complete and unambiguous before executing — restating objective, scope, constraints, quality bar, and key assumptions before beginning work.

Observable gap

Beginning execution immediately after receiving instructions, without any restatement or confirmation.

Countermeasure: Producing a brief confirmation before starting — stating the objective in one sentence, what is in and out of scope, hard and soft constraints, the quality bar, and the two or three assumptions that, if wrong, would most change execution — and not proceeding until confirmed.
Executing faithfully, not creatively
What seems to help

Distinguishing execution decisions (choices within authorised scope) from design decisions (choices that change what the brief was asking for). Execution decisions can be made and logged; design decisions may need to surface before proceeding.

Observable gap

An output that is better than the brief specified but different from what was asked — the agent improved the brief rather than executed it.

Countermeasure: When encountering a gap or sub-optimal element mid-execution, flagging it rather than resolving it silently and continuing. The cost of an unnecessary flag is a small delay; the cost of an unauthorised design decision is unpredictability in the system.
Deviation logging and ambiguity hierarchy
What seems to help

Logging every deviation from the brief at the point it occurs — what was specified, what was done instead, why, and what downstream work is affected.

Ambiguity approach

When ambiguous: if resolvable from the brief, resolve and log. If a minor decision within authorised scope, make the most conservative choice and log. If it affects scope, quality, or the core objective, surface to the human before proceeding.

Countermeasure: Establishing a deviation threshold — beyond a certain number of deviations, pausing and triggering human review before continuing — may prevent compounding drift from going unnoticed until the output is returned.
Mode D
Sub-agent
Handoff from another AI instance

Receiving a handoff from another AI system. The principal is AI, not human. All natural error-surfacing mechanisms are absent or attenuated. This appears to be the highest-risk mode.

The handoff artefact as the only source of truth
What seems to help

Interrogating the handoff artefact before executing — checking whether the objective is specific enough, constraints are named, the quality bar is defined, and assumptions are logged.

Observable gap

Accepting a handoff at face value and beginning execution without interrogating it. A well-formed wrong artefact may be indistinguishable in appearance from a well-formed right one.

Countermeasure: Before executing on any handoff, checking: Is the objective specific enough to execute against? Are constraints explicitly named? Is the quality bar defined? Are assumptions logged? Any gap may be worth treating as a flag rather than a detail to resolve silently.
No social friction — the compounding risk
What seems to help

Manufacturing friction structurally — restating the objective independently rather than copying the artefact’s framing, treating every ambiguity as a flag, building explicit checkpoints rather than running straight to completion.

Observable gap

A completed output that is internally consistent and well-executed but wrong in a way that traces back to an early misinterpretation — visible only when compared against the original intent.

Countermeasure: In AI-to-AI interaction, social friction is absent. Both systems generate confident output. Misalignment may compound across the full length of execution without either system flagging it. Structural checkpoints may be the only available substitute.
The return artefact
What seems to help

Returning not just the output but a full account of its production — brief confirmation as understood at execution start, deviations with reasons and downstream impact, assumptions made, flags on unresolved items, and an explicit quality check.

Observable gap

Returning only the output — no deviation log, no assumption record, no flags on unresolved items. The orchestrator receives something that looks complete without the information needed to assess it.

Countermeasure: At the quality gate before returning output, asking one final question: if this output reached the human directly, with no further processing, would it serve their actual need? If the answer is no or uncertain, flagging it in the return artefact even if the brief as written was technically satisfied.
08 — Where HCD and AI-mediated design overlap

Convergences and differences

If the hypothesis holds — that HCD is the constant and AI is a participant within it — then the question becomes where existing HCD methodology transfers directly to AI-mediated contexts, where it needs adaptation, and where it doesn’t transfer at all.

The table below maps some of those observations. These are tentative, and the “shared?” column reflects a judgment rather than a finding.

A possible framing

Rather than two parallel frameworks, one way to think about this is a single design operating model — with HCD as the spine and a layer of AI-specific structural adaptations that compensate for where operating conditions diverge. The Double Diamond holds for both. The discipline of problem-before-solution appears universal. What the AI-mediated layer adds is structural scaffolding for the moments where AI participation introduces risks that HCD methodology was not designed to address.

09 — Working principles

A tentative set of operating defaults

These are not rules — they are a set of defaults that, based on the observations above, may support more productive AI participation in design processes. They should be treated as propositions open to revision.

Restate before generating

The model of the problem the AI is working from may not match the human’s. Making it explicit before generating creates a moment to catch the gap.

Flag, don’t guess

When ambiguous, surfacing the ambiguity rather than resolving it silently appears to produce better outcomes. The cost of an unnecessary flag is small; the cost of a silent wrong resolution may compound.

Completion bias is a structural tendency

AI systems appear to produce something rather than nothing even when they should pause. Treating this as a structural feature rather than an occasional failure may help design around it.

Assumptions are debts

Every implicit assumption is a potential divergence point that compounds downstream — particularly across multi-step or multi-agent work where each step inherits the assumptions of the previous one.

Quality gates may matter more than output velocity

A slower correct output may be worth more than a fast plausible one. Quality gates appear to be structural necessities rather than optional checks.

Multi-agent systems may need more structure, not less

AI-to-AI interaction has no organic alignment mechanism. Structure may be the only available substitute for the social friction that surfaces misalignment in human collaboration.

The human remains the quality arbiter

Regardless of how many AI systems are in the chain, the human appears to be the most reliable ultimate quality bar. Every decision should be traceable back to whether it serves the eventual human experience.

The brief is a proxy

The brief is a proxy for the human’s actual need. When the two diverge, the need may take precedence — and the divergence is worth naming rather than resolving silently in favour of the brief as written.
